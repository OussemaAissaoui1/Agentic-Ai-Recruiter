    Introduction


In recent years, the integration of artificial intelligence and machine learning has pro-
foundly reshaped the way organisations manage their human-capital decisions. With
the rise of large language models, multimodal vision and speech encoders and agentic
frameworks, machines now understand documents, conduct conversations and analyse
behaviour with unprecedented fluency. Yet, despite this progress, the application of these
techniques to recruitment, one of the most consequential and most regulated decisions an
organisation makes, remains fragmented, shallow and largely opaque.

Hiring teams currently rely on a patchwork of disconnected products: applicant-tracking
systems perform keyword filtering, sourcing platforms expose closed proprietary scores,
and asynchronous video tools record candidates without ever adapting to them. At the
same time, the European AI Act and the NIST AI Risk Management Framework now
classify employment-related AI as high-risk and require a documented decision trail and
meaningful human oversight at every stage.

The goal of this project, titled HireFlow, is to design and deliver an end-to-end agentic
platform that supports a recruiter throughout the full hiring journey, from a vacancy
opening to the final hiring decision. Built around five collaborating agents responsible
for culture-aware job-description authoring, candidate–job matching, conversational in-
terviewing, multimodal behavioural analysis and recruiter-adaptive scoring, the system
leverages transformer models, graph neural networks and computer-vision pipelines while
preserving meaningful human oversight at every step.

This report is structured as follows. The first chapter situates the project, reviews existing
platforms and presents the proposed multi-agent solution. The remaining chapters follow
the Scrum cadence of the internship — one chapter per sprint — and the sprints that
build the artificial-intelligence agents are themselves organised around the six phases of
CRISP-DM. The final sections discuss the results, limitations and perspectives for future
improvement.

1.2        Project Overview

1.2.1      Project Context
Recruitment teams operate under intense pressure: open positions attract more applica-
tions than human reviewers can read, while candidates expect faster and more transparent
decisions. Regulators now require that hiring processes be auditable and that automated
decisions be explainable. The European Artificial Intelligence Act and the NIST AI Risk
Management Framework classify employment-related artificial intelligence as high-risk,
mandating a documented decision trail and meaningful human oversight at every stage.
In this environment, keyword-based applicant tracking is no longer sufficient, and the
profession is gradually reorganising itself around assistive AI tooling.

                                               3
Chapter 1 – General Context                                                              4


   The present project, conducted within the Innovation Factory, designs an end-to-end
agentic platform that supports a recruiter throughout the hiring journey, from a vacancy
opening to the final hiring decision. Rather than a monolithic application, the work
explores how independent specialised agents can collaborate to author the job descrip-
tion, screen incoming curricula, conduct the interview, analyse behaviour and produce an
explainable scoring report.


1.2.2       Problem Statement
Current hiring workflows exhibit several recurring shortcomings.
 • Generic, culture-blind job descriptions: most JDs are written from outdated
   templates and ignore what really characterises a successful hire in the company, often
   perpetuating biased phrasings.
 • Shallow candidate–job matching: keyword overlap fails to capture transferable
   skills, semantic equivalence between titles or context-dependent experience.
 • Limited interaction in asynchronous interviews: one-way video tools record
   candidates without follow-up questions or adaptation to the candidate’s profile.
 • Loss of behavioural signal in remote settings: engagement, hesitation and con-
   fidence cues that recruiters naturally read in person are discarded by current remote
   tooling.
 • Fragmented tooling: authoring, screening, interviewing and scoring live in discon-
   nected products, which prevents learning loops between stages.
 • Opacity and lack of personalisation: scores are presented as a single monolithic
   number and ignore the fact that different recruiters legitimately weight signals differ-
   ently.
These observations call for a unified system that combines culture-aware JD authoring,
deep semantic matching, adaptive conversational interviewing, multimodal behavioural
analysis and explainable, recruiter-aware scoring.


1.2.3       Objectives
 • Culture-aware JD authoring: an autonomous agent that grounds its generation
   in a knowledge graph of the company’s talent landscape, retrieves the relevant sub-
   graph through Graph RAG, and produces a personalised JD protected by a bias and
   compliance review.
 • Semantic matching: a model that fuses textual encoders, hierarchical attention and
   graph-based representations of the candidate–position bipartite structure.
 • Conversational interviewing: a virtual recruiter that grounds its dialogue in the
   candidate’s CV and decides between follow-up and topic-switching strategies.


                                            4
Chapter 1 – General Context                                                               5


 • Multimodal behavioural analysis: a low-dimensional profile extracted from video
   and audio streams, covering cognitive load, emotional arousal, engagement and confi-
   dence.
 • Objective scoring and reporting: structured post-interview reports combining
   transcript analysis, behavioural metrics and explicit hiring recommendations.
 • Recruiter-adaptive decision support: a personalised signal that learns each re-
   cruiter’s decision patterns while keeping the human in control.
 • Unified delivery platform: integrate the previous components into a single web
   application.


1.3     Review of Existing Solutions
This section positions the most representative platforms and highlights the gap that the
proposed system addresses.


1.3.1    Job Description Authoring
Textio and Ongig flag gendered terms and exclusionary expressions in an existing draft
and propose neutral alternatives. Their contribution stays at the stylistic-polishing layer:
neither generates the structural content of a description, neither taps into the company’s
workforce data, and neither manages the human-review checkpoints required by high-risk
AI regulation.


1.3.2    Sourcing and Candidate Matching
LinkedIn Recruiter exposes a powerful search engine over the LinkedIn graph, but its
matching logic is not customisable by the hiring team [URL4]. Eightfold AI encodes
profiles in a vector space supporting skill-based search and internal mobility, yet ships as
a closed enterprise product whose scoring internals are not exposed [URL2]. Manatal
and Workable bundle a candidate database with lightweight keyword-based matching
and compete on ergonomics rather than algorithmic depth [URL5].


1.3.3    Conversational and Asynchronous Interviewing
HireVue is the market reference for asynchronous video interviewing: candidates record
themselves answering predefined questions and the platform extracts features for auto-
mated rating [URL3]. The format scales but precludes any adaptation of the questions to
the candidate. Modern Hire (now part of HireVue) and myInterview share the same
non-conversational paradigm and the same criticisms regarding scoring transparency.

1.4        Proposed Solution
The proposed solution, internally named Agentic AI Recruiter, is a multi-agent platform
that orchestrates the full hiring journey under a single web application. Specialised agents
communicate through a lightweight agent-to-agent protocol exposed by a unified backend,
and the platform is structured around five operational stages summarised in Figure 1.1.
   (1) Job Description Authoring. A dedicated JD agent builds a knowledge graph
of the company’s talent landscape (employees, skills, projects, culture markers) and uses
Graph RAG to retrieve the relevant subgraph — past successful hires, shared skill clusters
and prevailing culture cues — which is then fed to a large language model that drafts a
personalised, culture-aware JD. The output is reviewed by a human through an evidence
panel before publication, in line with the EU AI Act’s human-oversight requirements. The
full multi-agent crew and its knowledge-graph backend are planned for the next iteration;
the current implementation already exposes a basic JD-generation endpoint.
   (2) Candidate–Job Matching. CVs and the generated JD are encoded with sen-
tence transformers, fed to a hierarchical-attention neural ranker and refined by a graph
neural network on the bipartite candidate–skill structure. The output is a ranked shortlist
that the recruiter validates.
   (3) Conversational Interview. A fine-tuned large language model drives the di-
alogue under a recruiter persona, generates contextual questions grounded in the can-

1.6.1      Why CRISP-DM?
CRISP-DM was preferred over a purely engineering-driven workflow because the AI com-
ponents of HireFlow are data-bound and require explicit handling of dataset assumptions,
leakage controls, distribution shift and offline evaluation. The same six phases were ap-
plied independently to each of the three models, while still leaving room for the back-and-

 • Business understanding: each model was framed against a concrete recruiter-facing

   LLM — before any data was collected.
 • Data understanding: exploratory analyses surfaced class imbalance, missing-skill

 • Deployment: each trained artefact was wrapped behind the same FastAPI agent
   contract, versioned and shadow-tested before replacing the previous model in produc-

The combination of Scrum at the project level and CRISP-DM at the data-science level
allowed managerial agility and methodological rigour to coexist throughout the internship.

language-processing techniques to build an end-to-end agentic recruitment assistant. This
chapter introduces the key concepts, architectures and protocols on which the platform is

2.2     Foundations of Artificial Intelligence and Natural
        Language Processing
This section recalls the building blocks shared by every other component of HireFlow: the
AI/ML/DL hierarchy, the three learning regimes, transfer learning, embeddings, and the
attention mechanism that underlies the transformer architecture.


2.2.1    Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning and Deep Learn-
         ing
Artificial intelligence (AI) is the broad scientific field concerned with the design of compu-
tational systems that perform tasks usually associated with human intelligence: percep-
tion, reasoning, decision-making, language understanding and creative production. Ma-
chine learning (ML) gathers the methods inside this field that improve their behaviour by
inferring patterns from data rather than by following hand-written rules. Deep learning
(DL) is the subset of machine learning that relies on artificial neural networks with many



                                             12
Chapter 2 – State of the Art                                                               13


layers, trained end-to-end by gradient descent, and is responsible for most of the progress
in vision, speech and language over the past decade.


2.2.2     Learning Regimes
Three learning regimes coexist in modern systems:
 • Supervised learning trains a model on input–output pairs (x, y) produced by human
   annotation, so that it learns to predict the correct y for an unseen x.
 • Self-supervised learning replaces the human label with a pretext task derived from the
   data itself, such as predicting the next token of a sentence or the masked word inside
   a paragraph; it is the dominant paradigm for pre-training large language and speech
   models on corpora too vast to annotate by hand.
 • Unsupervised learning discovers structure in unlabelled data through clustering, di-
   mensionality reduction or density estimation.


2.2.3     Pre-training, Fine-Tuning and Transfer Learning
Transfer learning reuses a model trained on one task to solve a related one. In modern
natural-language processing, the canonical form is pre-training a large model on a generic
self-supervised task with a very large corpus, then fine-tuning it on a small labelled dataset
that targets the application. Pre-training amortises the cost of learning general linguistic
competence, while fine-tuning specialises the model on the downstream task at a fraction
of that cost.


2.2.4     Tokenisation and Embeddings
A tokeniser converts a string into a sequence of integer identifiers from a fixed vocab-
ulary, usually through subword algorithms such as BPE, WordPiece or SentencePiece,
which eliminate out-of-vocabulary words. An embedding is a fixed-size dense vector that
represents a discrete object — a token, a sentence, a document or an entity — in a contin-
uous space where geometric proximity reflects semantic similarity. Sentence embeddings
aggregate the contextual embeddings of a whole sentence into a single vector, typically
by mean-pooling, and the cosine similarity between two such embeddings is the standard
semantic-distance measure.

from any other position, weighted by a learned compatibility score. In its scaled-dot-
product form, three projections of the input — the queries Q, the keys K and the values

2.3     Large Language Models and Fine-Tuning
This section presents the transformer architecture, the family of large language models
built on top of it, and the parameter-efficient methods that adapt them to specialised
tasks without re-training the billions of base weights.


2.3.1    The Transformer Architecture
Modern language models almost exclusively rely on the transformer architecture in-
troduced by Vaswani et al. in 2017 [7]. The transformer replaces sequential recurrence
with self-attention, which lifts a long-standing parallelism bottleneck and lets the model
capture dependencies between any two positions in constant computational depth. Fig-
ure 2.1 shows the canonical encoder–decoder architecture: the encoder ingests the source
sequence and produces a contextualised representation through stacked layers of multi-
head self-attention and feed-forward networks; the decoder generates the output sequence
autoregressively, combining masked self-attention with cross-attention over the encoder
output. Stacking identical blocks yields the encoder, the decoder, or, in the most common
contemporary configuration, a decoder-only stack specialised for next-token prediction.

2.3.2    Large Language Models
A large language model (LLM) is a transformer-based decoder-only network with billions
of parameters, pre-trained self-supervised on a multi-trillion-token corpus to predict the
next token, then instruction-tuned to follow human prompts. Open-weight families such
as Llama and Qwen make a class of techniques — inspection, fine-tuning, on-premises
deployment — accessible that closed commercial models do not.


2.3.3    Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning
Adapting an open-weight LLM to a specialised task no longer requires full fine-tuning
of the base model. The parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) family freezes the base
weights and trains a small number of additional parameters that capture the task-specific
adjustment. The dominant approach is LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) [4], which adds
to each linear layer a low-rank update

2.4     Multi-Agent Systems and Agent Protocols
This section defines what an agent is, presents the ReAct loop on which most modern agent
implementations are based, surveys the families of multi-agent orchestration frameworks,
and closes with the open communication protocols that connect them.


2.4.1    Agent Definition
An agent is an autonomous software entity built around a language model that, given
a goal expressed in natural language, decides on its own which actions to take, executes
them through external tools and observes the results before choosing the next step. Three
properties distinguish this paradigm from the prompt-and-response one. First, the agent
maintains an internal state across several steps, which lets it pursue a multi-step plan.
Second, it has access to a set of tools — search engines, databases, code interpreters,
custom APIs — that ground its reasoning in the external world and allow side effects
beyond text generation. Third, it autonomously decides when the task is complete and
returns control.


2.4.2    The ReAct Framework
The ReAct pattern (Reason + Act), introduced by Yao et al. in 2022 [8], is the canonical
instantiation of the agent loop. As sketched in Figure 2.2, the model alternates between
three kinds of step interleaved in a single autoregressive trace: Thought, in which it ver-
balises its reasoning in natural language; Action, in which it emits a structured tool call
parsed from its output; and Observation, in which the result of the tool call is fed back
into the prompt before the next thought. The loop continues until the agent decides
to commit to a final answer. Compared with chain-of-thought reasoning alone, the ex-
plicit observation channel grounds the chain in factual evidence and substantially reduces
hallucination on tasks that require external information.

2.4.3    Multi-Agent Orchestration
A single agent rarely covers the breadth of a real product. Three families of multi-agent
frameworks coexist:
 • Crew-based frameworks structure the collaboration as a team with explicit roles and
   a sequential or hierarchical workflow.
 • Conversational frameworks model agents as participants whose interactions are them-
   selves driven by a language model.
 • State-machine frameworks express the multi-agent process as a typed graph whose
   nodes are agent invocations and whose edges are conditional transitions, which makes
   the dialogue auditable and the branching deterministic.


2.4.4    Agent Communication Contracts
Two open specifications dominate the discussion:
 • The Model Context Protocol (MCP), introduced in late 2024, describes how a host
   application exposes tools, resources and prompts to a language-model-driven client.
 • The Agent-to-Agent (A2A) protocol, proposed in 2025, governs how peer agents dis-
   cover each other through AgentCards and exchange tasks and task results, typically
   over HTTP and JSON-RPC.
Both protocols decouple agent identity from transport, which makes the message contract
an independent design artefact.

2.5     Knowledge Graphs and Graph-Augmented Retrieval
This section defines knowledge graphs, recalls the standard retrieval-augmented-generation
recipe and presents Graph RAG, the two grounding mechanisms HireFlow uses to mitigate
hallucination.


2.5.1    Knowledge Graphs
A knowledge graph (KG) represents domain knowledge as a labelled directed graph in
which nodes are entities and edges are typed relationships, traditionally as subject–
predicate–object triples inherited from RDF. Knowledge graphs constrain the answers
of a generative model to facts present in the graph and expose multi-hop relationships
that a flat document index cannot represent.


2.5.2    Retrieval-Augmented Generation
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) injects external evidence into the prompt of a
language model at inference time. In its simplest form, the corpus is split into chunks,
each chunk is embedded with a sentence encoder and indexed in a vector store; at query
time, the top-k chunks are retrieved by cosine similarity and concatenated to the prompt.
Naive RAG performs well on short factual lookups but degrades on multi-hop questions
and on entity-centric queries.


2.5.3    Graph RAG
Graph RAG [3] replaces the flat chunk index with a graph of entities and their rela-
tionships extracted from the corpus by a language model and clustered into communities.
Retrieval becomes a graph traversal that returns a coherent subgraph, possibly augmented
with hierarchical community summaries, which measurably outperforms naive RAG on
global questions that require integrating information across many documents.


2.6     Candidate–Job Matching
This section traces the progression of candidate–job matching from bag-of-words retrieval
to dense semantic similarity, topic-aware hierarchical attention, graph neural networks
and constraint-aware shortlisting.

2.6.1       Sentence Embeddings and Semantic Similarity
The first generation of automated candidate screening relied on bag-of-words retrieval
(TF-IDF, BM25), which scores a resume against a job description by counting overlapping
terms and is therefore blind to synonymy and context. Sentence-BERT [6] replaces
lexical overlap with semantic similarity through a twin-encoder fine-tuning of BERT that
produces fixed-size sentence vectors directly comparable with cosine similarity, and the
family has since produced compact and multilingual variants used at scale.


2.6.2       Topic-Aware Person–Job Fit (TAPJFNN)
The person–job fit literature frames the matching problem as a binary classification: given
a job description and a resume, predict whether the candidate is a good fit. TAPJFNN
(Topic-Aware Person–Job Fit Neural Network ) [5] is the state-of-the-art representative of
the family. It combines a four-level hierarchical attention with a topic-aware mechanism in
which a corpus-level latent topic model modulates the higher attention weights so that the
network focuses on the sentences that align with the dominant topic of the job. Figure 2.3
sketches the four-level hierarchy applied symmetrically to the job and to the resume.

2.6.3       Latent Dirichlet Allocation
The topic distributions used by TAPJFNN are produced offline by Latent Dirichlet Allo-

mixture of a small number of latent topics and that each topic is in turn a distribution
over words; the two distributions are inferred jointly through approximate inference.

2.6.5      Genetic Algorithms for Constrained Shortlisting

2.7       Affective Computing and Speech Technologies

2.7.1       Models of Emotion
The affective-computing community models emotion in two complementary ways:
 • A discrete set of basic categories (happiness, sadness, fear, anger, surprise, disgust,
      contempt, neutral), traditionally attributed to Ekman.
 • A continuous space whose two dominant axes are valence (pleasantness) and arousal
      (activation), known as Russell’s circumplex.
The two representations coexist in production interview-analysis systems.


2.7.2       Facial Action Units and Landmarks
The FACS decomposes any facial expression into a small set of Action Units (AUs) corre-
sponding to individually contractible muscles (AU4 brow lowerer, AU12 lip corner puller).
AUs are typically estimated from facial landmarks — several hundred three-dimensional
points predicted on the face at thirty frames per second — rather than from raw pixels.
Pose-estimation and hand-tracking models complete the perception channel by capturing
body language and gesture.


2.7.3       Self-Supervised Speech Encoders
The dominant audio backbone today is wav2vec 2.0 [1], a self-supervised model that
learns speech representations directly from the raw audio waveform. A small downstream


                                               21
Chapter 2 – State of the Art                                                               22


head transforms its contextual embedding into a task-specific output: transcript, emotion
class, valence or arousal.


2.7.4     ASR, TTS and Voice-Activity Detection
The interview module relies on three complementary speech components:
 • ASR replaces the historical HMM pipeline with end-to-end encoder–decoder trans-
   formers trained on weakly supervised audio.
 • Neural TTS has moved from autoregressive seq-to-seq systems (Tacotron) to non-
   autoregressive flow-based models such as VITS that run faster than real time on
   commodity CPUs.
 • VAD decides whether speech is present in a short audio window; modern neural VAD
   replaces the WebRTC filter with a small recurrent network.


2.8     Personalised Ranking
A single global ranker is a poor approximation of any one recruiter’s preferences, since
two recruiters weight cultural fit, technical depth and communication style differently.
The most pragmatic personalisation model for a high-stakes hiring use case is logistic
regression: its coefficients are directly readable as feature weights and its calibrated prob-
ability output admits a natural confidence interpretation, which matters when the system
operates under regulatory constraints.

      3.1.4       Non-Functional Requirements
      The non-functional requirements derive from the dual nature of the platform: a research-
      grade multi-agent system that must nevertheless behave as a usable product.
       • Performance: end-to-end conversational latency below two seconds per turn; behavioural-
            signal pipeline running at no less than fifteen frames per second on a single GPU.
       • Scalability: stateless agent contracts that allow additional model instances to be
            added behind a load balancer without code changes.
       • Reliability: graceful degradation if a single agent fails — the recruiter must always
            reach a usable shortlist even when the conversational layer is unavailable.
       • Observability: every agent emits structured logs capturing the inputs, the version
            of the model that produced the decision and the timestamp at which it was emitted.
       • Usability: a recruiter with no AI background must be able to author a JD, launch
            an interview and read the scoring report without supervision.

 • Maintainability: each agent is a self-contained Python package with its own tests
   and contract so that components can evolve independently.
 • Portability: the platform runs on a developer laptop and on a single GPU node,
   without any external orchestrator dependency.


3.2     Project Planning with Scrum
HireFlow was delivered following a Scrum cadence adapted to the academic context of
an end-of-studies project. The methodology was chosen because it accommodates the
exploratory nature of multi-agent AI work — the precise shape of each agent emerges as
data and models are explored — while still imposing the discipline of two- to three-week
sprints, a written backlog and a demonstrable increment at the end of every iteration.

3.3      Application Architecture
The platform is built as a single deployable unit that nevertheless respects three classical
architectural views. Section 3.3.1 fixes the physical topology — which artefacts run on
which machine, with which protocols. Section 3.3.2 then reads the same system through
the logical lens of a three-tier web application: presentation, business, data. Section 3.3.3
zooms into the business tier to expose the multi-agent composition that gives HireFlow
its specificity.


3.3.1      Physical Architecture
The deployment is intentionally compact: one developer client, one GPU-equipped server,
and one external inference provider for fall-back generation. This keeps the platform
reproducible on a single Lightning AI Studio node, which was the constraint imposed by
the academic environment.

    shared Hugging Face cache for model weights.


    3.3.3        Logical Architecture of the Multi-Agent System
    Inside the business tier, HireFlow follows a hub-and-spoke multi-agent pattern. The Re-
    cruit Agent acts as the orchestrator: it receives the recruiter’s intent, decomposes it into
    specialised subtasks and routes each subtask to one of the five specialist agents. Commu-
    nication between agents follows the A2A protocol over JSON-RPC and is materialised
    on top of Redis streams, while each agent embeds its own models and tools (the talent
    knowledge graph queried through Graph RAG, vLLM, the GNN matcher, MediaPipe,
    Kokoro TTS, faster-whisper) directly.
